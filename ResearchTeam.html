Douglas M. Watts, M.S., Ph.D.
UTEP Principal Investigator

Dr. Watts has nearly 40 years experience conducting field and laboratory research on the ecology and epidemiology of zoonotic viral diseases.

Over his career with the Department of Defense, he held several key scientific and administrative positions that enabled him to acquire extensive experience in managing, advising, and directing research on enteric, parasitic and viral diseases in Asia, Africa, and South America.

During Dr. Wattsâ€™ tenure at the University of Texas Medical Branch (UTMB) at Galveston from 2002 to 2008, and in his current position as the Executive Director of Veterinary Services and Institutional Biosafety, and the Co-Director of the Border Biomedical Research Center at the University of Texas at El Paso (UTEP), he developed and evaluated candidate therapeutics and vaccines for Severe Acute Respiratory Disease Syndrome, dengue, yellow fever, monkey pox and West Nile viruses.

As the Principal Investigator of this project, he provides program guidance and technical leadership across the project, working closely with the Project Director to monitor and ensure that the project aims are achieved within the projected timeframe and budget.

Dr. Watts is experienced in select agent research and familiar with the ecology and epidemiology of Rift Valley fever, especially the history and status of vaccine development. He is also knowledgeable and proficient in RVFV laboratory methodologies. In addition to his overall responsibilities as the P.I., Dr. Watts will lead bioassay development to serologically distinguish animals that have been infected previously with RVF from those that were vaccinated with the MP-12-NSm-del vaccine. He will assist with the transfer of these research and development technologies to the Sokoine University of Agricultural site in Tanzania.

Dr. George Bettinger
UTEP Principal Investigator

Dr. Bettinger has managed product development in the healthcare industry for over 25 years, including serving as a plant manager.

He has lectured in Technical Project Management and Transfer of Products from R&D to Manufacturing for the American Management Association, and wrote and directed a successful project at UTMB to continue developing the MP-12 Rift Valley fever virus vaccine for human use through a Phase 2 clinical trial and established the manufacturing process for the vaccine at SAFC-Pharma.

He brings 40 years of successful international product and process development and management experience, including project management in both industry and academia to the project.

As the Project Director, Dr. Bettinger serves as the focal point for technical and operational matters for this project. He is responsible for integrating the development efforts across the multiple research centers at UTEP, UTMB, Sokoine University in Tanzania and the vaccine developer, MCI Sante-Animale in Morocco. Dr. Bettinger manages the flow of project information and reporting, and prepares the annual plans in compliance with USAID requirements.

Tetsuro Ikegami
Department of Pathology (Associate Professor)

Dr. Tetsuro Ikegami, DVM, Ph.D., a University of Texas Medical Branch (UTMB) associate professor and a Sealy Center for Vaccine Development member, who is part of a research team that is developing an improved vaccine for Rift Valley Fever virus.

Dr. Ikegami is the leader of the UTMB component of the USAID supported project to evaluate a novel RVF vaccine for the protection of livestock in Tanzania and his research is focused on the possibility that RVF vaccine genome could undergo reassortment or the exchange of genetic information with wild-type virulent strains of RVF virus, and/or with other related Phleboviruses to generate a more virulent vaccine virus and therefor present a safety concern.

Nandadeva Lokugamage, Ph.D.
Department of Pathology (Research Scientist)

Nandadeva Lokugamage, BVSc, PhD -- Dr. Lokugamage is responsible for preparing and analyzing RVFV samples to detect reassortant and recombination between two bunyaviruses.

He obtained his BVSc (DVM-equivalent) from The University of Peradeniya, Sri Lanka in 1991. Then, he received his PhD in Virology from Hokkaido University, Japan, in 2005.

He joined The University of Texas Medical Branch at Galveston (UTMB) as a Postdoctoral fellow in 2005, and studied genetic stability of Rift Valley fever virus (RVFV) MP-12 vaccine at Dr. C.J. Peters's laboratory.

In 2011, he was promoted to Research Scientist, and joined Dr. Ikegami's laboratory to further involve RVFV vaccine studies. Since then, he analyzed and published genetic subpopulations of MP-12 vaccine, parental ZH548 strain, and recombinant MP-12 strain using high-throughput sequencing in Journal of Virology.

He has 18 publications for bunyavirus studies. Currently, he is interested in genetic stability of individual MP-12 mutations, and operates droplet digital PCR to detect reversion mutations in different preparations of MP-12 vaccine. He trains Ms. Ly and other laboratory staffs for virology and molecular cloning to keep the productivity as a team.

Hoai Jaclyn Ly
Department of Pathology (Research technician)

Hoai J. Ly, MPH -- Ms. Ly is responsible for viral sample preparation, and co-infection studies using two different bunyaviruses for reassortant and recombination analyses. She also aims to develop reverse genetics system for Arumowot virus, which is a mosquito-borne phlebovirus in Africa.

Ms. Ly graduated from The University of Texas at Austin in 2012, and further earned MPH from Texas A&M University in May, 2014.

She joined Dr. Ikegami's laboratory as a Research Technician in December, 2013 to learn molecular virology using Rift Valley fever virus (RVFV).

She prepared large-scale virus stocks of rMP-12, rMP12-delNSm21-384, rMP12-TOSNSs, wild-type Arumowot virus, and wild-type Bunyamwera virus. Further, she successfully recovered and characterized viral replication kinetics of rMP12-GM50, which encodes silent mutations at each 50 nucleotide in open-reading frame of all genes.

She optimized conditions for cell culture of mosquito cells, and mouse infection model to start co-infection experiment, while developed a multiplex PCR to distinguish S-, M- and L-segment of MP-12 from those of rMP12-GM50 in a single PCR reaction. She wrote a first-author book chapter in Methods in Molecular Biology (Springer) about an application of droplet digital PCR in RVFV vaccine development in 2014. She is expected to be a graduate student at UTMB in 2015, and will further support the USAID project.

Project Leader

Professor Philemon Wambura, Ph.D. is a Professor of Virology in the School of Veterinary Medicine at Sokoine University, and serves as the local Project Leader.
